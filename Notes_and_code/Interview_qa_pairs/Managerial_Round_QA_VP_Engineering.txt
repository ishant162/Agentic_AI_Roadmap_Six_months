Ishant Thakare | AI Product Engineer

SECTION 1: ABOUT YOU & YOUR WORK STYLE
----------------------------------------------------------------

Q1. Tell me about yourself.

A: I'm an AI Engineer with 4 years at Capgemini, working across two domains — Federated
Learning and Agentic AI. On the FL side, I contributed directly to Intel's OpenFL open-source
framework — built the FederatedRuntime from scratch and designed the straggler handling
system, both merged into the official repo. On the agentic side, I've built production-grade
multi-agent systems using LangGraph — most recently a log analysis framework that reduced
manual debugging time by 80%. I'm now looking for a role where I can work closer to
product, owning AI workflows end to end rather than just the engineering piece.

---

Q2. What motivates you to switch from Capgemini?

A: Capgemini gave me strong technical depth — I got to work on real open-source systems,
not just internal tools. But the nature of consulting means you're often solving someone
else's problem statement. I want to be in a place where I'm shaping the problem, not just
executing on it. A product company where I can see the direct impact of what I build on
real users is where I want to grow next.

---

Q3. Where do you see yourself in 3 years?

A: I want to be the person who owns how AI works within a product — not just writing the
code but defining the architecture, the evaluation standards, and the guardrails. In 3 years
I'd like to be leading a small team building agentic systems, bridging the gap between what
the product needs and what AI can reliably deliver.

---

Q4. What kind of work environment brings out your best?

A: I work best when there's a clear problem but open space on how to solve it. I like
environments where ownership is real — where I can go from whiteboard to production
without too many handoffs. I also value direct feedback. I'd rather know something isn't
working early than find out after a month.

---

Q5. How do you prioritize when you have multiple deliverables at once?

A: I first separate what's blocking someone else from what only affects my own progress —
blocked dependencies go first. Then I assess reversibility — things that are hard to undo
get more time upfront. For my own work I timebox aggressively. If I'm stuck on something
for more than a few hours I either ask for help or move on and come back. I've learned that
perfect sequencing matters less than keeping things moving.

---

Q6. How do you manage your own learning alongside delivery?

A: I don't separate them. I learn by building — when I pick up a new framework or technique
I immediately apply it to a real problem, not a toy example. For staying current I follow
framework changelogs, read implementation-focused content, and contribute to open source
when I can. Code review from senior engineers in the community is the fastest feedback loop
I've found.

---

Q7. Describe your ideal manager.

A: Someone who gives me context on the why, sets clear expectations on the what, and then
gets out of the way on the how. I work best with managers who give direct feedback early
rather than saving it for reviews, and who trust me to escalate if I'm blocked rather than
checking in constantly.

---

Q8. What are your strengths?

A: Three things: I can go deep technically and also communicate to non-technical stakeholders
without losing the nuance. I take ownership — if something needs to be done and it's in my
vicinity, I do it without waiting to be asked. And I'm good at breaking ambiguous problems
into concrete steps — the FederatedRuntime and log analysis projects both started with vague
requirements and I drove them to production.

---

Q9. What are your weaknesses?

A: I have a tendency to over-engineer the first version — I want the architecture to be clean
before I ship. I've been actively working on this by setting explicit "good enough" criteria
before I start a task and timeboxing the first version. It's improved my shipping speed
significantly over the last year.

---

Q10. How do you handle ambiguity?

A: I anchor on the desired outcome and work backwards. When the FederatedRuntime spec was
essentially "make local and distributed code identical," there was no roadmap. I mapped what
the end-user experience needed to look like, identified the minimum components required to
achieve that, and built them in isolation before wiring together. Ambiguity is just a missing
map — you navigate by outcomes, not instructions.

----------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 2: LEADERSHIP & OWNERSHIP
----------------------------------------------------------------

Q11. Tell me about a time you took ownership of something beyond your job description.

A: During the straggler handling PR review, a maintainer pointed out that the percentage-based
policy had fundamentally incorrect logic. It wasn't my area to fix — but I understood the
codebase best at that point. I took it on, refactored the entire straggler handling module,
consolidated the policies, and filed the follow-up PR. Nobody asked me to. It needed to be
done and I was the right person to do it.

---

Q12. Have you ever disagreed with a technical decision made by your team? What did you do?

A: Yes. There was a discussion around using a fully synchronous aggregation approach for a
federated workflow that I felt would create bottlenecks at scale. I raised it with the lead,
backed it with a simple benchmark showing where it would break down, and proposed an async
alternative. The team agreed. My point was never "I'm right" — it was "here's data, let's
make an informed decision."

---

Q13. Tell me about a time you led something without having formal authority.

A: The FederatedRuntime project. I was not a tech lead but I designed the architecture, wrote
the implementation, handled review cycles with senior Intel maintainers, and drove it to merge.
Multiple rounds of feedback across time zones. I had to coordinate, defend design decisions,
and iterate — all without any formal authority. It shipped because I kept it moving.

---

Q14. Have you ever had to push back on a requirement from a stakeholder? What did you do?

A: Yes. There was a request to temporarily store raw collaborator data on the aggregator for
a feature. I pushed back — it violated the core privacy guarantee of federated learning. I
explained the risk clearly, proposed an alternative that achieved the same outcome without
the privacy compromise, and walked through the tradeoff. The stakeholder agreed once they
understood the implication.

---

Q15. Tell me about a time you mentored or helped a junior team member.

A: A junior engineer on my team was struggling with async patterns in Python — specifically
understanding why their LangGraph workflow was deadlocking. Instead of fixing it for them,
I walked them through how asyncio event loops work, reproduced the deadlock in a 10-line
example, and let them figure out the fix. They solved it themselves. The goal wasn't to
unblock the task — it was to make sure they wouldn't hit the same issue again.

---

Q16. How do you motivate yourself when working on a long, difficult project?

A: I break it into milestones where each one is a working, demonstrable thing. During
FederatedRuntime, instead of thinking "build the whole runtime," I focused on "get Director
to talk to Envoy via gRPC" as the first milestone. Each small working piece builds momentum.
When a project feels endless, it's usually because the next step isn't concrete enough.

---

Q17. Have you ever had to make a decision with incomplete information?

A: Constantly in distributed systems work. When debugging distributed-only bugs in
FederatedRuntime, I rarely had full visibility into what was happening across nodes. I had to
form a hypothesis, design the smallest experiment to test it, and act on partial evidence.
The key is knowing what you don't know and being explicit about your assumptions — so if
the hypothesis is wrong you can pivot fast rather than doubling down.

----------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 3: HANDLING CHALLENGES & FAILURE
----------------------------------------------------------------

Q18. Tell me about a time you failed. What did you learn?

A: Early in the straggler handling work, I shipped a version with a subtle timer lifecycle
bug — it passed local tests but failed in a multi-round distributed run. The PR review caught
it. What I learned: distributed systems fail in combinations. I now always test multi-round,
multi-node scenarios before marking something ready for review. Test the thing the way it
will actually be used.

---

Q19. Tell me about the most ambiguous problem you've worked on.

A: FederatedRuntime. The requirement was "make local and distributed experiments use the
same code." No spec, no prior implementation. I started from the user experience — one line
change, same experiment runs anywhere — and worked backwards into the architecture.
When you don't have a clear path, anchor on the outcome and reverse engineer to the steps.

---

Q20. Describe a time when a project went off track. What did you do?

A: During FederatedRuntime, distributed-only bugs were appearing faster than I could fix them
— connection drops, checkpoint failures, workspace errors, all at once. I stopped trying to
fix everything in parallel. I triaged strictly by severity — what's blocking a complete run
vs. what's a nice-to-fix. Fixed the blockers, got a full end-to-end run working, then
addressed the rest. Momentum matters more than perfection when a project is slipping.

---

Q21. Tell me about a time you had to deliver under pressure.

A: The straggler handling follow-up PR — reviewers explicitly said the PR was taking too long
and asked to close it that week. I had multiple open review comments, regression tests to
run, and documentation to update. I time-boxed each comment, prioritized blockers for merge,
communicated clearly with reviewers on status, and got it across the line. Pressure is
manageable when you're transparent about progress.

---

Q22. Tell me about a time you had to quickly learn something new to solve a problem.

A: Building FederatedRuntime required gRPC and mTLS from scratch — I had never built a
full gRPC service before. I didn't wait until I "knew it well enough." I read the core concepts,
built a minimal Director-Envoy handshake to validate my understanding, and iterated from
there. The fastest way to learn a new technology is to build something real with it
immediately, not to study it in isolation.

---

Q23. Describe a situation where you had to deal with a difficult technical reviewer or stakeholder.

A: During the OpenFL PRs, one reviewer flagged a fundamental design issue late in the review
cycle — the percentage-based straggler policy had incorrect logic. It was a valid point but
came late and required significant rework. I didn't push back on the timing — I acknowledged
it, scoped the fix clearly, and filed a separate follow-up PR to keep the original moving.
Separate what's a blocker for merge from what can be addressed in a follow-up. Keep the
critical path short.

---

Q24. How do you handle a situation where you are blocked and can't make progress?

A: I timebox the block — if I haven't made progress in 2-3 hours I stop and either ask for
help or switch to a parallel task. I also document what I've tried before asking, so whoever
helps me can give a useful answer immediately. Being blocked is not a failure — staying
blocked silently is.

----------------------------------------------------------------
SECTION 4: COLLABORATION & COMMUNICATION
----------------------------------------------------------------

Q25. How do you work with non-technical stakeholders?

A: I anchor on outcomes, not implementation. If a stakeholder asks "can the agent handle
this?" I don't explain attention mechanisms — I say "yes, here's what it will do, here's
where it might fail, here's the tradeoff." I also set expectations early on what AI can and
can't do reliably, so there are no surprises in production.

---

Q26. Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a teammate. How was it resolved?

A: There was a design disagreement on the memory architecture for my log analysis project.
A colleague felt a single vector store was sufficient; I felt layered memory was necessary
for rare error patterns. Rather than debating abstractly, I ran a quick experiment — tested
both on 50 real log cases and shared the results. Layered memory performed significantly
better on rare errors. Evidence ended the debate faster than discussion would have.

---

Q27. How do you explain complex AI concepts to business or product teams?

A: I use outcomes and analogies. For HITL: "it's a checkpoint where a human approves before
the agent acts — like a draft email waiting for you to hit send." For RAG: "it's giving the
AI a relevant reference document before asking it a question, so it answers from facts not
guesswork." I never start with the architecture. I start with what it means for the user.

---

Q28. How do you handle feedback you disagree with?

A: I engage with it seriously before deciding whether to push back. In my OpenFL PRs I got
several review comments I initially disagreed with — in most cases the reviewer was right
when I thought it through. When I genuinely disagreed I stated my reasoning with evidence
and let the maintainer decide. I've never dug in just for the sake of it.

---

Q29. How do you communicate progress on a project to leadership?

A: Short, regular updates anchored on status against milestones — what's done, what's next,
what's blocked. I don't wait until something is perfect to share it. Early visibility on
blockers is more valuable than a polished update that comes too late to act on.

---

Q30. Tell me about a time you had to collaborate with people across different time zones or cultures.

A: The OpenFL PR reviews involved maintainers from Intel in the US and reviewers in Europe.
Feedback cycles were slow — a comment would come in at midnight my time. I adapted by
front-loading my questions, anticipating follow-up feedback, and batching responses so each
async cycle moved the PR forward as much as possible. Async collaboration works when you
treat each exchange as high bandwidth — not just answering the question asked but closing
the next two questions as well.

---

Q31. How do you give feedback to a peer when their work is not up to standard?

A: Directly and specifically. "This won't work in a multi-round scenario because of X" is
more useful than "I think this needs more work." I focus on the problem, not the person,
and I always pair the feedback with either a concrete suggestion or an offer to work through
it together. I also do it privately first — not in a PR comment thread where others are watching.

SECTION 5: IMPACT & SCALE THINKING
----------------------------------------------------------------

Q32. What's the most impactful thing you've shipped? How do you measure impact?

A: FederatedRuntime in OpenFL. It's open source, merged into an Intel-backed framework used
by researchers globally. The impact: researchers no longer need to rewrite experiments to
deploy them — one line change, same code runs distributed. I measure it by adoption and by
the absence of friction. If nobody's asking "how do I deploy this?" the abstraction is working.

---

Q33. How do you think about building things that scale vs. shipping fast?

A: I don't treat them as opposites. I ship fast by scoping tightly — build the smallest thing
that validates the core assumption. I build for scale by not making decisions that are hard
to undo — clean interfaces, no shortcuts in state management, proper error handling from
day one. The expensive mistakes aren't the features you didn't build fast enough — they're
the architectural decisions you can't refactor later.

---

Q34. How do you decide when something is "good enough" to ship?

A: When it handles the happy path reliably, all known failure modes have a defined handler,
and I've tested it against real inputs not just synthetic ones. I also ask: if this breaks in
production, can I diagnose it quickly? If the answer is no, it's not ready — observability
is part of done, not a follow-up task.

---

Q35. How do you approach technical debt?

A: I distinguish between intentional debt — shortcuts taken consciously to ship faster with
a plan to fix — and accidental debt — poor decisions made without awareness. Intentional
debt is fine if it's documented and scheduled. Accidental debt compounds silently and
eventually blocks everything. I try to leave every codebase slightly cleaner than I found it,
and I flag debt explicitly in PRs so it doesn't get forgotten.

---

Q36. How do you evaluate whether a new technology or tool is worth adopting?

A: Three questions: Does it solve a real problem I have right now? Is it mature enough for
production, or will I spend more time fighting the framework than building? What's the cost
of switching away from it if it doesn't work out? I prototype with real data before committing.
Hype is not a reason to adopt. A concrete improvement in reliability, speed, or developer
experience is.

---

Q37. Tell me about a time you improved a process, not just a product.

A: In my FL work, the team had no structured way to reproduce distributed bugs — every
engineer set up their own environment differently. I built a 4-node WSL-based local test
environment and documented it as a standard setup. After that, distributed bugs could be
reproduced consistently by anyone on the team, not just whoever happened to have the right
setup. Debugging time for distributed issues dropped significantly.

Q38. Why this company specifically?

A: The intersection of cybersecurity and agentic AI is genuinely hard — it's not a domain
where you can ship a demo and call it a product. The failure modes are real and consequential.
My background in FL gave me experience building systems where trust, reliability, and
controlled autonomy matter — privacy guarantees, fault tolerance, audit trails. That maps
directly to what this role needs.

---

Q39. What do you know about our product and where do you think AI can add value?

A: *(Customize this based on your research on the company before the interview.)*

The highest-value AI play in security/SRE is reducing time-to-triage and time-to-resolution —
not by replacing the analyst but by handling the first 80% of the investigation autonomously:
enrichment, correlation, pattern matching against past incidents, and surfacing the right
context before the human even looks at it. The agent doesn't need to be right 100% of the
time — it needs to be right enough to cut analyst workload and fast enough to matter.

---

Q40. What does good look like for this role in the first 90 days?

A: First 30 days: understand the product deeply — the workflows, the users, the pain points.
Shadow actual operators if possible. First 60 days: ship one working agentic workflow end
to end, even if small. Prove the pattern works in this environment. First 90 days: have a
clear view of the two or three highest-value workflow automations and a concrete plan to
build them. Good in 90 days means I've earned trust by shipping something real, not just
producing decks.

---

Q41. How do you stay current in a field that moves as fast as AI?

A: I build things. Reading papers tells you what exists — building tells you what works.
I follow framework changelogs closely — LangGraph, LangChain, OpenAI — because production
behavior changes faster than documentation. I contribute to open source when I can, because
code review from senior engineers in the community is the fastest way to level up.

---

Q42. What's one thing about AI agents that most people get wrong?

A: They think the hard part is the LLM. It's not. The LLM is the easiest part — you call an
API and it reasons. The hard parts are state management, failure handling, observability, and
knowing when NOT to use an agent. Most failed agent projects fail because they tried to
automate something that needed more determinism, or because there was no plan for what
happens when the agent is wrong. Trust is built through guardrails and HITL, not through
a better model.

---

Q43. What would you do in the first week if you joined?

A: Listen and read. Understand the product from a user's perspective before forming opinions.
Talk to the engineers who've been there longest — they know where the bodies are buried.
Read closed issues and past PRs to understand what's been tried and why it was or wasn't
done. I'd resist the urge to propose solutions in the first week. The best thing a new person
can do early is ask good questions, not give answers.

---

Q44. How do you think about the balance between autonomy and control in AI systems?

A: Autonomy should be earned incrementally. Start with the agent in read-only mode —
it observes and suggests, humans act. Measure accuracy. Once trust is established, unlock
low-risk write actions with audit logging. High-risk actions stay behind a HITL checkpoint
indefinitely — not because the model isn't capable, but because the cost of being wrong is
too high to automate. Autonomy without observability is a liability, not a feature.

---

Q45. Where do you think AI in cybersecurity is headed in the next 2 years?

A: The shift from AI-assisted to AI-initiated. Right now most tools surface information and
let humans act. In 2 years the leading products will have agents that take action —
containing threats, isolating endpoints, executing remediation — with humans in an approval
loop rather than an execution loop. The companies that will win are the ones building the
trust infrastructure now: explainability, audit trails, guardrails, and rollback capability.
The model capability is largely there. The trust layer is what's missing.

---

Q46. How do you handle working on something where you're not the domain expert?

A: I rely heavily on the domain experts around me early, and I ask specific questions not
generic ones. When I built the health coach project, I had no clinical background. I focused
on what I could own — the AI architecture and the integration layer — and made sure every
clinical assumption was validated by someone who knew the domain. I'm not afraid of not
knowing a domain. I'm afraid of not knowing that I don't know it.

---

Q47. What do you think makes an AI product trustworthy?

A: Four things: it does what it says it will do (reliability), it tells you when it's not sure
(calibration), it leaves a clear trail of every decision it made (auditability), and it fails
gracefully rather than silently (fault tolerance). Trust isn't about the model being smart.
It's about the system being predictable and honest about its limits.

---

Q48. Tell me something you built that you're genuinely proud of.

A: FederatedRuntime in OpenFL. Not because it was technically complex — though it was —
but because it solved a real friction point for the FL research community. The fact that it
was reviewed by senior engineers at Intel, went through multiple rounds of rigorous review,
and was merged into a production open-source framework used globally — that means more
to me than any internal project. It will outlast my time at Capgemini and be used by people
I'll never meet.

---

Q49. How do you think about your own career growth?

A: I optimize for learning rate, not title. The roles where I've grown fastest were the ones
where I was slightly out of my depth — building things I hadn't built before, being reviewed
by people who were better than me. I'm not chasing a specific title in 3 years. I'm chasing
the problems that will make me significantly better at this craft.
